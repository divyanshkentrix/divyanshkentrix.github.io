The Narrative
Not all branches are created equal — and comparing them on raw performance alone is misleading. The real question is: given what each catchment offers, how much is each branch actually capturing?
This analysis inverts the traditional benchmarking lens. Instead of ranking branches by profit and asking "who's winning?", we start from the catchment — the 920M+ consumer profiles mapped by Kentrix at building-level precision — and ask: what should each branch be doing, given its market?

For every product line (SB, CA, Term Deposits, Gold Loans, MSME, SRA/Home Loans), we estimate a catchment-derived demand potential using household income distribution, financial savings propensity, real estate ownership patterns, commercial activity density, and jewellery holding segments. This potential is then adjusted for competitive displacement — accounting for the presence and tier of competitor banks — to arrive at an addressable opportunity.

The ratio of actual performance to addressable opportunity gives us Conversion Efficiency — the true measure of branch effectiveness. A branch doing ₹25 Cr in a ₹500 Cr market is underperforming. A branch doing ₹11 Cr in a ₹19 Cr market is extraordinary.
⚡
The biggest puzzle in this portfolio is Ahmedabad-VL. It sits in the richest catchment across all 9 branches — 94.7% affluent households, ₹2.45L average monthly income, ₹39.2k financial savings spend — yet captures only 16.7% of its addressable potential. With just 4 staff and ₹0.7 Cr profit, this is either a capacity-starved branch or a product-market mismatch waiting to be fixed. The gap is ₹448 Cr across deposit and loan products.
🏆
Godda defies its catchment. With zero affluent households and a demand index near the bottom on every metric, it generates ₹11 Cr profit — 19x its estimated potential. This is likely a legacy franchise effect, a government/institutional deposit base, or a regional monopoly. Whatever the cause, the model that works in Godda cannot be replicated elsewhere — it's an anomaly, not a template.
📊
Meerut has the largest absolute opportunity gap. With 58,019 catchment households, the highest FD propensity score (100/100), and the highest SRA demand index (100/100), yet only ₹326 Cr total book against ₹810 Cr addressable potential. The ₹328 Cr SRA gap alone is larger than many branches' entire book. With 12 staff already deployed, this is a product push problem, not a capacity problem.
Catchment DNA
Each branch serves a fundamentally different market. Before comparing performance, we must understand what each catchment looks like — its income structure, lifestyle segments, and banking propensity.
The income pyramid tells you who lives here. The LSI (Lifestyle Segmentation Intelligence) tells you how they live. And the demand indices tell you what financial products they need. A branch in an LSI-01 dominated catchment (affluent urban) needs wealth management and premium SRA products. A branch in LSI-05/06 territory (lower middle/semi-urban) needs gold loans and basic savings accounts. Misalignment between catchment DNA and product offering is where revenue leaks.
Potential vs. Actual — The Gap Analysis
For each product line, the teal bar shows addressable potential (after competitive displacement), and the colored bar shows actual performance. The gap is revenue being left on the table.
Demand potential is estimated bottom-up: SB from household income distribution × financial savings propensity × banking usage intensity. CA from commercial activity (office locations, employee counts). Term Deposits from households in high financial-savings bands × FD propensity. Gold Loans from mid-tier gold jewellery holding segments. MSME from commercial density and SME proxies. SRA (Home/Personal Loans) from real estate propensity in the ₹25L–4Cr bands. All potentials are then discounted by a Weighted Competitive Index (Tier-1 banks weighted 1.0, Tier-2 at 0.6, Tier-3 at 0.3) and an IDBI brand factor.
Conversion Efficiency
Actual ÷ Addressable Potential × 100. This is the true measure of branch effectiveness — how much of what's available is being captured.
A conversion rate above 100% means the branch is outperforming its estimated catchment potential — either through a broader draw area, institutional deposits, or a model our catchment-level estimation doesn't fully capture. A rate below 50% signals significant untapped opportunity. The branches that matter most are those with low conversion in high-potential catchments — that's where the highest ROI interventions live.
🎯
Conversion efficiency varies 10x across branches. Ahmedabad-VL converts just 6.3% of SB potential vs. Udaipur at 297%. The highest-impact intervention targets are branches with high potential + low conversion: Ahmedabad-VL (SB, TD), Meerut (TD, SRA), and Bilaspur (SRA, CA).
Product-Market Fit
Is each branch selling the right products for its catchment? PMF Score measures the alignment between what the market demands and what the branch actually delivers.
Product-Market Fit is calculated as the cosine similarity between the catchment demand vector (SB, CA, TD, Gold, MSME, SRA potentials) and the actual portfolio vector. A score of 1.0 = perfect alignment. A low PMF means the branch portfolio is structurally different from what its catchment demands — this is a strategic misallocation, not just an execution problem. Low PMF + Low Conversion = the catchment wants products the branch isn't pushing.
🔄
Kandi (PMF: 0.43) and Ramanathapuram (0.45) have the worst product-market fit. Both catchments have high SRA demand indices driven by real estate propensity, but their actual SRA books are thin. Kandi captures just 7.8% of SRA potential, Ramanathapuram just 14.4%. A focused home loan push in these markets could materially change branch economics.
Competitive Landscape
Not all competitors displace equally. The Weighted Competitive Index weights Tier-1 banks (SBI, HDFC, ICICI, Axis, Kotak) at 1.0, Tier-2 at 0.6, and Tier-3 at 0.3.
Delhi-NP faces the most intense competition (WCI: 92.1) with 17 HDFC branches, 16 SBI, 11 ICICI, and 12 Axis in its catchment. Yet it leads on profit. The competitive displacement factor reduces its addressable potential to just 31.5% of raw demand — but what it captures from that reduced pool is exceptional. Contrast with Godda (WCI: 1.6) — near-zero competition, giving IDBI an effective monopoly and explaining why it outperforms its raw catchment by 19x.
